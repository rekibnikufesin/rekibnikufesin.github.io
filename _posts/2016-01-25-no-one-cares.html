Without resorting to references about living under a rock or being from the stone age, it’s a fair assumption that you’re familiar with Amazon’s AWS API Gateway and Lambda. Maybe you’ve even logged in and checked it out. At that point though, if you didn’t dedicate some serious book-learnin' time to it, you probably hit the same high learning curve that has stopped many from pursuing it further.

No shit. It’s a seriously steep learning curve. Especially if you weren’t already familiar with the AWS Console. So that raises the question: why bother? You’ve already got some means of deploying your API servers and have enough “new stuff” to learn as it is (especially if you’re coding in JavaScript!). Let’s look at what we don’t have to do when you deploy using the API Gateway and Lambda.

Patching: everyone who loves reading Release Notes, verifying dependencies and validating compatibility across versions raise your hand! Hmmm. Not a big fan, huh? Yeah, me neither.

Maintenance: Routine maintenance windows with scheduled downtime or rolling updates? Yes please, I’d love 0 more of those!

Hardware: BOOM! I love getting failed disk alerts at 2am and rolling my nocturnal ass down to a data center to change out a hard drive! Not.

Clustering, load balancing, deployment integration, continuous deployment: all great, worthy stuff and a necessity to online business today, but if I can get out of that work???

In my opinion, that’s the real beauty of services like the API Gateway and Lambda. Your application is launched automatically on a fully patched, fault tolerant, scalable instance and no effort is required on your part to make that happen. Well, no effort aside from learning how to use the tool. That’s a fair trade. The biggest benefit of all though is that you spend more time writing the code that delivers value to your customers. Sure, all of the things I listed above are of value to your customer, but it’s not the value they want to pay you for. They want to pay you for the application logic that enables them to do that thing they do, that’s why your their guy or girl, not because you have a stack of spare hard drives next to your night stand.

So yeah, I totally agree the learning curve is steep. But with a little effort on your part, it turns into faster delivery and potentially better service for your customers.
